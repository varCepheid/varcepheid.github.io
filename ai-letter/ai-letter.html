Educators, Please Let Students Use AI, the Right Way

Alex Vennebush
19 May 2026

To high school teachers and college professors across the United States:

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) is an incredible classroom tool. It has spread across the education landscape so quickly, though, that no one knows the most appropriate ways to use it. You want to see your students learn and have the skills to succeed in their future classes, educational endeavors, or careers, but it can feel like AI tools get in the way of these objectives. How do you make AI work in the classroom while preparing students effectively? As a student, I offer this solution: Let your students use AI. But show them how to use it the right way.

Plenty of educators, writers, and parents have advocated for a total ban on generative AI for students. There are good reasons why, but this is a flawed approach.

Research has shown benefits for students using AI tools. For language learning, generative AI is invaluable for practice and quick feedback. It helps break down complicated concepts in simple language. Students perform better and learn faster when using AI.

More importantly, banning AI entirely is counterproductive. The future is uncertain, but no one doubts that generative AI will play a huge role in real-world work in many industries going forward. An accounting professor would never ask students to complete assignments without a calculator. Why, then, would you take AI out of your students’ toolbox, when it may be the most important tool they will have in the workplace?

What the critics get right is that unimpeded access to generative AI for students is clearly a bad idea. Like any other tool, students need to learn how to use AI effectively and well without undermining their learning.

To continue the analogy, calculators have become a limiting crutch to many students. Many of my classmates, so accustomed to having calculators nearby, cannot do even basic arithmetic in their heads. In the same way, you should not give your students complete freedom with AI, but limit them to appropriate uses.

Research shows the concrete negative effects of generative AI use. Students who consistently use AI perform worse on critical-thinking assessments and on other neural, linguistic, and behavioral benchmarks. They suffer from cognitive offloading, letting external tools do their thinking because it is easier on their minds.

How, then, in the age of AI, can you prevent your students from participating in cognitive offloading and help them build those critical mental skills?

Technology and cognition expert John Nosta offers one approach to this problem. He identifies basically two phases in the development of a skill: learning the basics of the skill, and reinforcing it through practice. Thinking, then doing.

Turning to generative AI during the learning phase is cognitive offloading. Instead of doing the mental work of learning the skill, the student creates the appearance of performance by passing off AI output as their own, without really learning anything. We should not let students use AI this way.

Making generative AI accessible in the reinforcement phase, however, can be extremely valuable to make the student stronger at the skill and more comfortable in their ability. Instead of impairing the student’s growth, AI use strengthens it by providing the feedback and motivation to keep going.

This could show up in a number of ways. In an English class, for example, Nosta suggests that students could write a draft — the cognitively intense part of writing, usually — and then allow an AI chatbot to critique the draft and help the student find stronger arguments or more compelling phrasing. It is not taking away the thinking; it challenges the student to think more. In Nosta’s imagined math or science class, students attempt problems on their own but use generative AI to check their work. The student has to have the basis of the skill already, and feedback from the AI makes that skill stronger. Even in a fine-arts class, generative AI could help students get inspiration for their projects and find sources for their research without denying them the opportunity to build skills and exercise their creativity.

In every classroom, it should be clear to students that AI tools are prohibited for the learning and thinking stage but allowed for the reinforcement stage. A strong way to do this is to demonstrate, in front of students, the acceptable ways to use generative AI. It should be an integrated part of classroom instruction, since it is an important tool for students to use. You know best how to help your students succeed, depending on the course and the individual students, but there is always space to apply this approach to AI use.

In addition, schools and universities should teach the skills that generative AI cannot take away. The skills that AI cannot do will be the most important ones going forward. No one is sure exactly which skills this will be, but two are quite likely: creativity and critical thinking.

The arts have been losing funding recently, but they will become newly important in an age where creation no longer requires creativity. AI-generated “slop” is so easy to make that real human art, with emotion and passion, will gain value from novelty and a new recognition of the effort it takes. And with AI tools able to automate the technical components of many jobs, the creative skills to complement them will become even more necessary in the AI-driven workforce, even in non-artistic fields.

Simultaneously, critical-thinking skills will become even more valuable. Every student has to navigate a media landscape permeated by AI-generated headlines and a populace willing to offload their cognitive abilities to chatbots. Media literacy and its associated skills will be more important than ever.

Curricula should reorient, where possible, to make these skills a focus of all students’ educational paths. Require fine arts classes, and encourage students to pursue the classical liberal arts, such as debate and creative writing. Create more discussion-based courses, which require students to think critically, craft arguments, and analyze different points of view.

By reorienting your classes and curricula to the reality of generative AI, you give your students the best opportunity to succeed in the workplaces of the future. Set them up for success with both the tools and the cognitive abilities they will need. Educators, let your students use AI — the right way.

With eternal gratitude,
var Cepheid
